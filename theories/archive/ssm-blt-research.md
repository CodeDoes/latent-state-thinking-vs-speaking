# Beyond Tokens: State Space Models at the Intersection of Byte-Level Representation and End-to-End Learning

## The Conceptual Framework of Byte Latent Encoding

The investigation into the application of byte latent encoding to state space models (SSMs) reveals that while the term itself is not a monolithic, formally defined technique, it represents a significant conceptual shift in sequence modeling [[4,5]]. Researchers are moving away from the conventional paradigm of training models on discrete tokens generated by a separate tokenizer, towards architectures that learn directly from raw byte sequences [[2,7]]. This approach aims to eliminate the biases, preprocessing overhead, and vocabulary limitations inherent in tokenization methods like Byte Pair Encoding (BPE), which are standard in models such as Transformers [[2,8]]. Instead of relying on an external step to convert data into a manageable set of symbols, "byte latent encoding" describes the process of learning meaningful, compact representations directly from the fundamental units of digital information—the byte. The provided research materials show this concept being implemented through several distinct architectural philosophies, each tailored to address the challenges of operating on longer, less structured sequences.

One primary manifestation of this concept is the development of pure byte-level models that operate autoregressively on raw byte streams without any form of explicit grouping or tokenization [[7]]. These models treat every byte in a file, such as a text document encoded in UTF-8, as a sequential input element [[6]]. A canonical example of this approach is MambaByte, which adapts the Mamba selective state space model to function as a token-free language model [[1,2]]. In this paradigm, the entire sequence of bytes becomes the input, and the model learns to predict the next byte in the sequence. This strategy fundamentally changes the nature of the problem, transforming a task where the model operates on a vocabulary of thousands or millions of tokens into one where it must navigate a much larger sequence of 256 possible byte values [[1]]. The core innovation lies in the model architecture's ability to handle this increased sequence length efficiently, a challenge that has historically plagued standard autoregressive Transformers due to their quadratic computational complexity with respect to sequence length [[2]].

A second, more modular interpretation involves using a dedicated encoder to first transform the raw byte stream into a more abstract, information-rich representation before passing it to an SSM for sequence modeling [[5]]. This approach decouples the initial encoding step from the sequential processing step. The "Byte Latent Representations" (BLR) framework exemplifies this design, where a byte-level convolutional encoder is tasked with learning spectral–spatial encodings directly from the byte data without requiring explicit tokenization [[5]]. The output of this encoder—a series of dense vectors representing patches of the original data—is then fed into a Selective State-Space Model (SSM) to capture long-range dependencies [[5]]. This hybrid architecture leverages the strengths of different components: the convolutional encoder excels at local feature extraction and compression, while the SSM is adept at modeling global structure and temporal relationships in the resulting sequence of embeddings. This method allows for a more nuanced handling of data, potentially preserving more semantic information than a simple byte-by-byte processing model.

A third category of models introduces dynamic chunking or patching mechanisms, where the model itself learns to segment the byte stream into variable-sized patches based on local data complexity [[8,9]]. These patches, which can be thought of as adaptive tokens, are then processed by the main network, which can include SSMs. The H-Net model is a prime example, proposing an end-to-end trainable system that replaces the traditional tokenizer-language model pipeline [[8]]. Its key innovation is a dynamic chunking mechanism that learns content- and context-dependent segmentation strategies without heuristics or external supervision [[8]]. Within this hierarchical structure, the authors explicitly choose to use Mamba-2 layers as the sequence mixing component, highlighting the suitability of SSMs for processing the semi-structured outputs of such a chunking module [[8]]. Similarly, the TimeSqueeze model employs a Mamba-based encoder-decoder to generate fine-grained embeddings from raw time series data, followed by a dynamic patching module that allocates smaller patches to information-rich regions and larger ones to redundant segments [[9]]. This approach shares conceptual similarities with the Byte Latent Transformer (BLT), which also uses entropy-guided segmentation to merge predictable byte spans into compact latent tokens [[4,9]]. In all these cases, the SSM acts as a powerful engine for sequence modeling, but its role is often secondary to a higher-level mechanism that first organizes or compresses the byte-level data.

The table below summarizes the key distinctions between these three architectural interpretations of byte-level modeling, illustrating that "byte latent encoding" is best understood as an umbrella concept encompassing multiple innovative strategies rather than a single, prescribed method.

| Feature | Direct Byte Processing (e.g., MambaByte) | Encoder-SSM Hybrid (e.g., BLM-KD) | Dynamic Chunking/SSM (e.g., H-Net) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Input Handling** | Processes raw byte sequences autoregressively [[2]]. | Uses a specialized encoder (e.g., convolutional) to create latent representations from bytes [[5]]. | Employs a learned chunking mechanism to group bytes into variable-sized patches [[8]]. |
| **Role of SSM** | Primary sequence model operating directly on bytes [[1]]. | Secondary component that processes the latent representations generated by the encoder [[5]]. | Component used for sequence mixing within a larger hierarchical architecture [[8]]. |
| **Tokenization** | No tokenization; operates at the raw byte level [[6]]. | No explicit tokenization; encoding is implicit in the encoder's output [[5]]. | Replaces fixed-tokenization with learned, adaptive "chunks" or "patches" [[8]]. |
| **Primary Advantage** | Simplicity and robustness to noise [[1]]. | Effective for domains with complex spatial-spectral features (e.g., images) [[5]]. | Flexibility to allocate compute based on local data complexity [[9]]. |
| **Representative Model** | MambaByte [[6]]. | Byte Latent Mamba (BLM-KD) [[5]]. | H-Net [[8]]. |

In essence, the pursuit of byte-level representation demonstrates a broader trend in machine learning towards more end-to-end systems that can learn directly from source data formats. By bypassing the intermediate step of tokenization, these models aim to build more fundamental and generalizable representations of information, leveraging the unique strengths of SSMs—namely linear scaling and efficient long-sequence processing—to overcome the computational hurdles associated with working at the byte level [[1,2]]. The diversity of approaches shows that the research community is actively exploring various ways to integrate byte-level understanding into modern neural architectures, with SSMs emerging as a central and versatile tool in this endeavor.

## Direct Byte-Level Processing with MambaByte

The most direct and prominent application of a state space model to byte-level data is embodied in the MambaByte architecture [[1,6]]. MambaByte is a token-free adaptation of the Mamba selective state space model, specifically designed to learn directly from raw byte sequences without the need for subword tokenization [[1,2]]. This approach addresses a fundamental limitation of standard autoregressive Transformers when applied to long sequences, such as those produced by operating on bytes instead of tokens [[1]]. The motivation for MambaByte stems from the observation that while Transformers scale poorly with sequence length due to their quadratic computational complexity, SSMs like Mamba offer linear scaling, making them inherently better suited for such tasks [[2]]. Furthermore, the fixed-sized memory state of SSMs provides an efficient mechanism for processing the significantly longer sequences that arise from byte-level inputs [[1]].

The core innovation of MambaByte is its autoregressive training on raw byte sequences, typically encoded in UTF-8 [[2]]. This eliminates the bias introduced by subword tokenizers like BPE, which can struggle with languages, code, and other modalities where word boundaries are ambiguous or non-existent [[2,8]]. By operating directly on bytes, MambaByte avoids creating a vocabulary that may not align well with the underlying semantics of the data, leading to a more robust model that is less susceptible to noise and out-of-vocabulary issues [[1]]. The technical implementation reflects this design philosophy; a code snippet from the project's repository demonstrates converting a string to its UTF-8 byte representation using `np.frombuffer(text.encode('utf-8'), dtype=np.uint8)` and passing this byte array directly to the model as `input_ids`, bypassing any embedding lookup table associated with tokens [[6]]. This stands in contrast to the typical usage of the official Mamba repository, whose documentation examples show it accepting tensor inputs of shape `[batch, length, dim]` derived from high-level token embeddings, implying a standard Transformer-like workflow [[3]]. The existence of MambaByte thus appears to be a specialized adaptation rather than a built-in feature of the core library, requiring a custom data pipeline to feed raw bytes into the SSM architecture [[3,6]].

Despite the theoretical challenges of processing longer sequences, experimental results have shown that MambaByte is highly competitive with, and in some cases outperforms, state-of-the-art subword-tokenized Transformers on language modeling tasks [[2]]. This success validates the feasibility of the token-free paradigm and highlights the efficiency gains offered by the SSM architecture. The model maintains the benefits of token-free models, such as robustness to noise, while achieving performance comparable to or exceeding that of models trained on subwords [[1]]. The potential applications extend beyond traditional text. The authors of the MambaByte paper suggest that the model could be adapted for multimodal tasks, potentially reading and writing popular file formats like UTF-8, UTF-32, Latin-1, EBCDIC, WAV, BMP, TIFF, RAW, PNG, JPG, and ZIP by treating them as byte sequences [[2]]. This versatility underscores the power of a token-free approach, which is not tied to the syntactic conventions of a specific modality like natural language.

Further enhancing the practicality of MambaByte, an efficiency improvement called speculative decoding with tokenized drafting and byte-level verification was developed [[1]]. This technique resulted in a 2.6x inference speedup compared to the standard MambaByte implementation, addressing one of the primary concerns about the real-world deployment of models that process longer sequences [[1]]. This optimization demonstrates a concerted effort to make byte-level models not only theoretically sound but also practically viable. The availability of pretrained MambaByte models, such as 'MambaByte_Code', 'MambaByte_Stories', and versions trained on PG-19 datasets, further solidifies its status as a concrete and actively developed implementation of a token-free SSM [[6]]. The work on MambaByte, documented in a preprint on arXiv, represents a clear and compelling case study of how SSMs can be successfully and effectively applied to the challenging but promising domain of byte-level sequence modeling [[6]]. It serves as a foundational example of the broader movement to create more flexible and efficient sequence models that operate closer to the raw data format.

## Encoder-SSM Hybrid Architectures: The Byte Latent Mamba Case Study

Beyond direct byte processing, another significant application of byte-level concepts within SSM frameworks is found in hybrid architectures that use a dedicated encoder to generate latent representations from raw bytes before feeding them into an SSM. The Byte Latent Mamba with Knowledge Distillation (BLM-KD) model, proposed for hyperspectral image classification (HSIC), provides a detailed and explicit example of this approach [[5]]. This model integrates a Selective State-Space Model (SSM) into a comprehensive pipeline that begins with a byte-level convolutional encoder, demonstrating a sophisticated method for learning from data at its most fundamental level [[5]]. The core innovation of BLM-KD is the use of 'Byte Latent Representations' (BLR), which are designed to learn byte-level spectral–spatial encodings without the need for explicit tokenization [[5]]. This directly aligns with the user's query, offering a concrete instance of a "byte latent encoding" mechanism being used as a precursor to an SSM.

The architecture of BLM-KD consists of four main components: a byte-level convolutional encoder, a Selective State-Space Model (SSM), a classification head, and an adaptive knowledge distillation module [[5]]. The initial convolutional encoder plays a crucial role by processing the raw byte data and learning to extract relevant features. This step is vital because hyperspectral images contain rich spectral and spatial information that is not easily captured by simply treating the flattened image data as a sequence of bytes. The encoder transforms the high-dimensional byte stream into a more compact and semantically meaningful representation. Following this, the Selective State-Space Model (SSM) takes over, responsible for capturing the long-range dependencies present in the data generated by the encoder [[5]]. This choice is strategically sound, as SSMs are particularly effective at modeling long-range correlations in sequential data, a common characteristic of hyperspectral image spectra. The final components, the classification head and the knowledge distillation module, complete the pipeline for the specific task of image classification [[5]].

This hybrid design showcases the modularity of modern deep learning architectures, where different types of neural networks are combined to leverage their respective strengths. The convolutional encoder excels at local feature extraction, a task for which it is classically well-suited, while the SSM is adept at global sequence modeling. This division of labor allows the overall system to be more effective than if a single architecture were forced to perform both tasks. The paper, accepted for publication in the IEEE Transactions on Geoscience and Remote Sensing, confirms the viability of this approach in a demanding scientific domain [[5]]. The success of BLM-KD in HSIC suggests that the "byte latent" approach may be particularly powerful in scenarios where data naturally exists in a multi-dimensional format that can be reduced to a sequence of byte values. This contrasts with the language modeling focus of MambaByte, indicating that the utility of byte-level SSM architectures can be highly dependent on the specific characteristics of the data domain.

The BLM-KD model provides a clear illustration of how the concept of "byte latent encoding" can be operationalized. The term itself refers to the byte-level convolutional encoder's function of learning byte-level spectral–spatial encodings without explicit tokenization [[5]]. This learned representation serves as the input to the SSM, positioning the SSM as a critical component for subsequent sequence analysis. The full name of the model, "Byte Latent Mamba," reinforces the direct connection between the byte-level encoding mechanism and the SSM that follows it [[5]]. While the provided sources do not delve into extensive comparative benchmarks against tokenized models for this specific task, the publication in a reputable, specialized journal indicates that the research community finds this architectural fusion to be a novel and valuable contribution. The BLM-KD case study is therefore a key piece of evidence confirming that byte-level concepts are being actively integrated with SSMs, albeit through a more complex, encoder-based pathway than the direct processing seen in MambaByte. It highlights the flexibility of the SSM as a versatile component that can be inserted into diverse pipelines to enhance performance on tasks involving byte-level data.

## SSMs as Components in Hierarchical Byte-Level Models

The application of state space models extends beyond standalone architectures like MambaByte or dedicated encoder-SSM hybrids like BLM-KD. In a more advanced paradigm, SSMs serve as powerful, interchangeable components within larger, hierarchical models designed specifically for byte-level processing. This approach leverages the unique strengths of SSMs—particularly their linear scaling and ability to model long-range dependencies—as part of a more complex system that first organizes or compresses the raw byte stream. The H-Net and TimeSqueeze models are prominent examples of this strategy, with H-Net explicitly integrating Mamba layers into its architecture [[8,9]]. This demonstrates a trend towards modular design, where the SSM is treated as a high-performance engine for sequence mixing, plugged into a broader framework that handles the initial stages of byte-level abstraction.

The H-Net model proposes a radical departure from the standard NLP pipeline of tokenization, language modeling, and detokenization [[8]]. Instead of relying on an external tokenizer, H-Net is an end-to-end trainable system that learns its own segmentation of the data. Its core innovation is the "dynamic chunking" (DC) mechanism, which learns content- and context-dependent segmentation strategies directly from the data without any heuristics or external supervision [[8]]. This module uses a routing mechanism based on cosine similarity to predict segmentation boundaries and a smoothing module to ensure the process remains differentiable for training purposes [[8]]. The chunks created by this mechanism can be thought of as adaptive, context-aware tokens. Crucially, the main network within H-Net is designed to be flexible and can utilize any standard sequence mixing architecture. The authors of the paper specifically highlight that SSMs like Mamba-2 align well with the strengths of Transformers in processing discrete, semantically-rich tokens, making them an excellent fit for this role [[8]]. They implement their main network using Mamba-2 layers, demonstrating that an SSM can effectively process the semi-structured, chunked output of a higher-level algorithm. This choice is motivated by research showing SSMs excel at processing fine-grained data and possess a stronger inductive bias for compression, which is essential for building abstractions from raw bytes [[8]].

Similarly, the TimeSqueeze model presents a hybrid tokenization mechanism designed for time series data [[9]]. It combines a lightweight state-space encoder-decoder with dynamic patching to extract and compress fine-grained features. The architecture uses Mamba layers as both the encoder and decoder [[9]]. The process begins with the Mamba-based encoder generating full-resolution point-wise embeddings from the raw time series input. These embeddings are then passed to a dynamic patching module that groups them into patches of varying sizes, allocating smaller patches to information-rich regions and larger patches to more redundant segments [[9]]. The use of Mamba layers in this dual capacity—both for feature extraction and for enabling the patching mechanism—underscores their versatility. The paper draws a parallel to the Byte Latent Transformer (BLT), noting that both models aim to adaptively compress fine-grained data before processing, although they operate in different domains [[9]]. The explicit reference to BLT in the discussion of tokenizer-free language models situates TimeSqueeze within the same conceptual family, even though its application is to time series rather than text [[9]].

These hierarchical models showcase a sophisticated use of SSMs. Rather than being the sole architect of the model, the SSM becomes a highly capable tool within a more complex system. This modular approach offers several advantages. First, it separates the difficult problem of finding optimal data segmentation from the problem of sequence modeling. Second, it allows researchers to benefit from the rapid advancements in SSM technology without having to redesign the entire system from scratch. Third, it enables the creation of models that are more aligned with the intrinsic structure of certain data types, such as time series or images, where a simple linear sequence of tokens is a poor representation. The fact that both H-Net and TimeSqueeze identify SSMs as a preferred component for their sequence-mixing tasks highlights a strong consensus in the research community regarding the effectiveness of SSMs for this purpose [[8,9]]. This positions SSMs not just as an alternative to Transformers, but as a complementary and indispensable building block for the next generation of byte-level and token-free models.

## Strategic Motivations and Comparative Advantages

The concerted effort to integrate byte-level concepts with state space models is driven by a set of powerful strategic motivations aimed at overcoming the inherent limitations of the dominant token-based paradigm, primarily associated with Transformers. The primary impetus is the elimination of tokenizer bias [[2,8]]. Subword tokenizers like BPE, while effective for many natural languages, introduce a layer of approximation that can be problematic. They rely on a predefined vocabulary and heuristic merging rules, which can lead to poor performance on morphologically rich languages, programming code, DNA sequences, and other domains with weak tokenization cues [[7,8]]. By operating directly on raw bytes, models like MambaByte and the H-Net architecture bypass this step entirely, allowing the model to learn more fundamental and unbiased representations of the data from its native format [[1,8]]. This approach treats all data uniformly, whether it is text, executable code, or an image file, providing a path toward more general-purpose and cross-modal models [[2]].

A second major driver is the pursuit of improved computational efficiency and scalability, particularly for long sequences [[1,2]]. Standard autoregressive Transformers suffer from a quadratic increase in computational cost and memory requirements as sequence length grows, making them impractical for tasks involving very long contexts or high-resolution data [[1]]. Operating on bytes dramatically increases sequence length—for example, a typical text sentence might become dozens or hundreds of bytes long—exacerbating this problem. SSMs, and specifically the Mamba variant, offer a solution with their linear scaling properties, meaning their computational cost grows proportionally with sequence length [[2]]. This makes it feasible to train and deploy models on byte-level data where Transformers would fail. The development of speculative decoding techniques for MambaByte, which yielded a 2.6x inference speedup, further illustrates the focus on optimizing the practical efficiency of these models [[1]]. This efficiency is not just a matter of faster computation; it opens up new application areas that were previously inaccessible due to hardware constraints.

Beyond efficiency and bias reduction, proponents of byte-level modeling argue for enhanced robustness and generalization capabilities. Models that do not rely on a fixed vocabulary are inherently more robust to noise, misspellings, and variations in the input data, as they are not constrained by what is or isn't in their token dictionary [[1]]. There is also evidence suggesting that these models exhibit superior reasoning abilities and long-tail generalization [[4]]. The Byte Latent Transformer (BLT) scaling study, which involved models up to 8 billion parameters trained on 8 trillion bytes, demonstrated that end-to-end training from raw bytes is not only possible but can yield qualitative improvements in reasoning tasks [[4]]. The argument is that by forcing the model to learn abstractions directly from the lowest level of data representation, it discovers more fundamental patterns and structures that may be obscured or lost during the tokenization process [[8]]. This can lead to better performance on tasks that require true understanding rather than pattern matching within a limited vocabulary.

Collectively, these motivations paint a picture of a research direction focused on creating more flexible, efficient, and powerful sequence models. By combining the linear-scaling properties of SSMs with the conceptual purity of byte-level processing, researchers are developing models that are better equipped to handle the complexities of real-world data. While the provided materials do not contain exhaustive comparative benchmarks against highly optimized tokenized models across a wide range of tasks, the consistent articulation of these strategic advantages across multiple independent research efforts suggests a strong belief in the long-term potential of this approach. The focus is not merely on incremental improvement but on rethinking the foundational assumptions of how machines learn from sequential information.

## Synthesis and Identified Research Gaps

The comprehensive analysis of the provided research materials confirms that state space models (SSMs) have indeed been applied to byte-level data, moving beyond the conventional token-based paradigm. This intersection is not characterized by a single, monolithic technique called "byte latent encoding," but rather by a collection of innovative and conceptually related architectural strategies. These approaches share a common goal: to enable models to learn directly from raw byte sequences, thereby eliminating the biases and inefficiencies of traditional tokenization. The evidence points to three primary manifestations of this integration. The first is the development of pure byte-level SSMs, with MambaByte serving as the definitive example of a token-free model that operates autoregressively on raw byte streams [[1,6]]. The second is the creation of hybrid architectures, such as the Byte Latent Mamba with Knowledge Distillation (BLM-KD), which uses a dedicated byte-level encoder to generate latent representations before passing them to an SSM for sequence modeling [[5]]. The third approach involves using SSMs as a core component within more complex, hierarchical models like H-Net and TimeSqueeze, where they process data that has been organized into variable-sized chunks or patches by a higher-level algorithm [[8,9]]. Across these diverse implementations, the SSM consistently proves to be a versatile and powerful tool for sequence analysis at the byte level, valued for its linear scaling properties that make long-sequence modeling computationally tractable [[2]].

The strategic motivations underpinning this research are compelling and multifaceted. The primary drivers are the desire to eliminate the arbitrary biases introduced by subword tokenizers, improve computational efficiency for long sequences, and enhance model robustness and generalization capabilities [[1,2,4]]. By operating closer to the source data format, these models promise to be more flexible and better suited for a wider range of modalities, including text, code, DNA sequences, and various file formats [[2,8]]. The successful demonstration of these concepts in models like MambaByte, which competes with state-of-the-art tokenized Transformers, and BLM-KD, which achieves strong results in a scientific domain, validates the feasibility and potential of this research direction [[2,5]]. The active development and publication of these models indicate that the intersection of SSMs and byte-level representation is a vibrant and promising area of contemporary machine learning research.

Despite the clear progress and promising results, several gaps and areas for further inquiry remain apparent from the provided sources. First, there is a lack of a unified definition and taxonomy for the various "byte latent encoding" techniques. The distinction between direct SSMs, encoder-SSM hybrids, and hierarchical chunking models is significant, yet they are often discussed under a similar conceptual umbrella [[7,8]]. Future work could benefit from a more standardized classification of these methods, which would help clarify their respective strengths, weaknesses, and ideal application domains. Second, while the computational advantages are noted, detailed and extensive comparative benchmarks against highly optimized tokenized models across a broad spectrum of tasks are not fully detailed. Understanding the precise trade-offs in terms of training time, memory consumption, inference latency, and final performance on downstream tasks is crucial for widespread adoption. Third, the scalability and data requirements for training these more complex models are not thoroughly explored. The mention of scaling a Byte Latent Transformer to 8 billion parameters suggests substantial resources are needed, and the costs associated with models incorporating dynamic chunking or specialized encoders are likely significant [[4]]. Finally, the generalizability of these architectures remains an open question. The demonstrated success of BLM-KD in hyperspectral image classification does not guarantee its effectiveness in other domains, nor does the success of MambaByte in language modeling directly translate to time-series analysis without careful architectural consideration [[5,9]]. Addressing these gaps will be essential for maturing this field and unlocking the full potential of sequence models that learn directly from the byte.
